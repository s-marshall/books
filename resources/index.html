Translating Japanese Books People Liked



Few works are translated into English from any foreign language. My puny contribution to fill this void is the translation of popular Japanese literature in the public domain. Vast stores of Japanese literature can be found online at Aozora Bunko and the National Diet Library Digital Collections (NDL).


I translate what I find interesting through a combination of reading about Japanese authors and works on the Web, book reviews on Amazon Japan, word searches on the NDL, and flukes. The works I have translated range from a samurai manga to the recollections of an influential twentieth-century scientist. Future books include a bilingual text of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde and more samurai novels.

With only boredom to fight, he solves the mysteries behind murders and kidnappings, and battles corrupt officials. He thirsts for justice, and his sword for blood.

Dazai Osamu killed himself. While some of his writings reflected his feelings of isolation and his self-medication, he also wrote humorous and uplifting stories. These books are two of his lighthearted tales. Pandora's Box details the experiences of a twenty-year-old youth in a sanatorium in post-war Japan. Goodbye was his unfinished last work about a man's machinations to break up with his dozen or so lovers. He committed suicide with his lover; the two are pictured on the cover.

Despite the early death of Miyazawa Kenji and only an unfinished manuscript left behind, Night on the Milky Way Railroad has become a children's classic in Japan. A boy, Giovanni, has few friends at school, a sick mother, and a father working far from home. One festival night, he is swept onto a train traveling the Milky Way and into a fantastic world.
